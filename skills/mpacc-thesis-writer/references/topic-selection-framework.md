# Topic Selection Framework

Use this before proposing or accepting an MPAcc topic. The purpose is not to make a title sound advanced; it is to find a real accounting, finance, audit, management-accounting, tax, internal-control, risk, governance, or related professional problem that can be researched and solved with available evidence.

## Selection Order

1. Define the topic type before choosing samples: true-problem, tool-application, or institutional-difference. The strongest route is often true-problem plus tool-application.
2. State the reality observation: what contrast, anomaly, deterioration, regulatory event, disclosure issue, process failure, cost deviation, risk exposure, or decision problem is visible?
3. Convert the observation into one research question: `observable phenomenon + one or two key associated factors + consequence`.
4. Give a provisional answer/main claim. It must be a defensible relationship or mechanism, not the reversed wording of the problem.
5. Select the official thesis form: case analysis report, thematic research paper, survey report, scheme design, or product design.
6. Select the analytical pattern: optimization, mechanism/effect, model/accounting treatment, event/spillover, configuration, staged path, or intelligent-technology application.
7. Select the case design and evidence boundary. Only then write candidate titles.

## Hard Reject Gates

Reject or rewrite the topic if any gate fails:

- The problem is not directly visible in standard financial indicators, public operating indicators, regulatory files, inquiry letters, bond documents, litigation, public disclosures, or verifiable case materials.
- The core concept is invented or decorative and has no literature root or management-practice root.
- The narrative contradicts the data direction. If indicators improve, state improvement and then analyze sufficiency, sustainability, or structural cost; do not label it as deterioration.
- The causal chain is only an accounting identity or common sense, such as "negative FCF -> borrowing -> cash shortage" or "gross margin falls -> profit falls".
- The accounting/professional line disappears behind technology, law, policy, strategy, or general management.
- The topic depends on non-public internal decisions, contracts, ledgers, credit files, or meeting minutes for its core conclusion.
- The same sample set has required three or more core-concept replacements. Change the sample or topic type instead of repackaging the concept.

## Topic Types

| Type | Use when | Required evidence |
|---|---|---|
| True-problem | The case has a visible financial, operating, debt, governance, disclosure, audit, cost, budget, risk, or control problem | Standard indicators or public events show the problem without reconstruction |
| Tool-application | A management-accounting, financial-management, risk-control, audit, budget, performance, or resource-allocation tool can solve a concrete problem | Current tool/process weakness, implementation path, industry-specific indicators, threshold basis |
| Institutional-difference | Similar organizations show different outcomes because of governance, financing, customer structure, supply-chain organization, internal control, budget mechanism, or risk appetite | Comparable evidence and a non-trivial mechanism linking arrangement to outcome |

## Research Question Test

Before accepting a topic, answer in one or two sentences:

- What reality problem is visible?
- What key factor(s) may explain it?
- What consequence or decision does it affect?
- Who can use the answer, and what practical problem will it help solve?

Good: "A data-center company shows persistent negative FCF, rising debt pressure, and weak cash coverage during expansion; the study asks how project payback period and debt maturity mismatch amplify liquidity risk, and how an industry-specific KRI tool can set warning thresholds."

Weak: "Study financial risk in data-center companies under the digital economy."

## Case Design Rules

Single-case designs should be one of:

- Extreme/unusual: the case is visibly different from common practice or regulation.
- Illuminating: the case reveals a new phenomenon or hard-to-observe setting.
- Longitudinal: the case can be tracked by stages with changing mechanisms.

Multi-case designs should be one of:

- Competitive: similar background, different paths, differentiated outcomes.
- Equifinal: different starting points or paths, similar outcomes.
- Two-tail: opposite extreme cases.
- Variation: controls irrelevant differences and focuses on the key variable.

For competitive designs, be honest about ownership, customer strategy, business model, financing channel, life-cycle stage, and other differences. Do not pretend case comparison is strict causal identification. Use comparability, difference, and process tracing to build an explanatory mechanism.

## Tool-Application Standards

For KRI, budget warning, risk map, project post-evaluation, performance evaluation, or similar tools:

- Make the tool industry-specific; do not copy generic ratios.
- Give threshold bases: accounting standards, disclosure rules, industry benchmarks, rating-agency criteria, bank-credit conditions, regulatory focus, or peer practice.
- Specify owner, data path, trigger action, and expected effect.
- Do not claim statistical prediction or ex post causal validation if the tool is only a management warning system.

## Scoring

After hard gates pass, score each item 0-2:

- Real problem visibility.
- Accounting/professional relevance.
- Evidence availability and source credibility.
- Analytical depth beyond identities and common sense.
- Case design fit.
- Practical solution feasibility.
- Literature or practice root for core concepts.
- Clear boundary: object, period, industry, and problem.

Scores below 12 require narrowing or replacement. Any hard-gate failure overrides a high score.

## Title Pattern

Prefer `context/tool + case/object + professional problem + action`.

Good: `基于行业化 KRI 的 A 数据中心企业债务期限错配风险管控研究`

Weak: `双碳背景下企业绿色发展研究`

Quality gate: the selected topic must state what is being explained or improved, where evidence comes from, which professional decision it supports, and what solution or insight it can produce.
